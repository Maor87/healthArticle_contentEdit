THE NEW HEALTH REVOLUTION!

Growing up, Dinesh Fernando reveled in the fizz of his beloved sugary drinks, blind to the hidden sugar content. Over time, his fondness for these sweet beverages spiraled into a daunting diabetes battle. Dinesh's tale is sadly not unique in Sri Lanka.

Sri Lanka, for the first time in the country, new health measures will be passed in by law for sugary beverage products to help the public make better health decisions.

The New Color System

The Ministry of Health announced, "From August 1, it will be mandatory to smack color codes to all soft drinks based on their sugar content percentage." This initiative, known as traffic light labelling, will instantly inform consumers if the sugar quantity in a soft drink is high, medium, or low.

Red will be displayed if the sugar content is more than 12 grams per 100 milliltres, while amber points to a dose of between 2g and 12g. Green denotes that the drink has a sugar content of less than 2g. The government plans to introduce a similar color coding system, to expose salt and fat content in various types of food.

Big Change's

The latest measures, based on World Health Organization guidelines for healthy sugar consumption, have been in the works for five years. The plans were unveiled by the minister for health nutrition and indigenous medicine, Dr. Rajitha Senaratne.

Under the new law's, cafes, food outlets and restaurants will have to offer sugar and salt separately instead of adding them to the meal or hot drink. Minister Senaratne said: 'taxes on sugar will also increase as part of a package of new health boosting measures'.

Free to Pick

Color codes will appear alongside other information on food and drink items, such as the list of ingredients, details about their nutritional value, allergy warnings, and expiry dates. The changes are aimed to making it easier for people to pick healthier drink options.

With green indicating low amounts, it is the healthier choice. With amber indicating medium amounts, one may gulp down food and drink with ambers, most of the time.

Some Push Back

The ministry for health said: 'A meeting was held with soft drink manufacturers registered under the Sri Lanka Chamber of Commerce about the implementation of color coding'. Still, not everyone supports the government's latest measures.

'Some soft-drink manufactures have said they were against the proposals, however still legally ablogated', said the minister. One european beverage company even considered exiting the Sri Lankan market as a result. The government was prompted to act as the nation's health continues to worsen.

Some health professionals say the daily sugar requirements is between 10g and 12g, or two teaspoons, per day whereas the average Sri Lankan consumes about 60g per day.

The Raise of Diabetes

A survey carried out in the Western Province had revealed that the sugar consumption level there, particularly among children, was higher than it was on average, across all Sri Lankan provinces. Children who are overweight are more likely to develop illnesses such as Type-2 diabetes, have an increased likelihood of weight and health problems in adolescence, and are more likely to become overweight or obese adults.

'Over 70% of hospital patients suffer from diseases caused by excessive consumption of sugar, salt, and fat. These measures are part of a broader government effort to reverse this troubling trend and improve Sri Lankan health'. The minister of health said.

The minister also said: 'Another of his ambitions is to make Sri Lanka's health care system as good as others in more developed countries'. The objective of this regulation was to make it easier for people to make better choices based on how healthy a particular drink is.

Let Dinesh's story be our wake-up call. With this new initiative, we envision a healthier Sri Lanka, where tales of sugar induced illnesses become rare. Together, we can embrace this change and secure a healthier future.
